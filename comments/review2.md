Review #1192B
Paper summary (3-4 sentences)
The paper does two related things. First, a controlled label-fidelity study over 10 ChiPBench circuits asks which intermediate flow stage gives supervision labels that actually rank placements consistently with post-route PPA: HPWL and pre-CTS STA correlate near-zero with post-DRT timing, post-CTS is inconsistent, and post-GRT is the cost/fidelity sweet spot. Second, building on this, PPAPlace trains a dual-stream predictor (GAT over the netlist + CNN over a 64x64 spatial grid with RUDY congestion) on post-GRT labels and uses its gradients in two deployment modes: CoOpt injects the surrogate into DREAMPlace's analytical loop as a co-objective with a warmup-and-ramp schedule; Refine runs projected gradient descent on macro positions of any converged placement. On 5 held-out ChiPBench circuits the combined mode beats every reported baseline including Hier-RTLMP, Re2MaP.

Strengths
The label-fidelity study is a useful empirical contribution on its own. It directly indicts the supervision choice used by the nearest competitor (LaMPlace) and gives future cross-stage prediction work a principled answer for where to put its supervision.
The key numbers are measured through the actual OpenROAD post-DRT flow, not a proxy. The surrogate is trained on post-GRT and the test metrics are post-DRT, so the claim that GRT-trained gradients carry through to detailed routing is empirically supported.
The ablation in Table 5 factorially separates the two contributions. This is helpful for the reader.
Gradient quality is validated directly (Table 3: cosine similarity 0.53/0.46 against true post-GRT perturbations on 100 random directions per circuit), and the OOD failure mode is acknowledged with Fig 3(b).
Power is roughly flat (0.99-1.02x) and routability is preserved (zero DRC violations, routed wirelength within 3% of default), so the WNS/TNS gains are not tradeoffs (which is great).
Weaknesses
Cross-placer transfer is weaker than within-placer (rho 0.61 vs 0.77, Table 4 right), which matters because Refine is claimed to be placer-agnostic. The training data is DREAMPlace placements, the label-fidelity study is RTLMP placements, and the paper says this is not a mismatch because fidelity is a flow property. That argument is right to some extent but does not address whether the predictor learns features specific to DREAMPlace's output distribution. The cross-placer numbers suggest it does, to some degree.
Three of the five test circuits share lineage with training circuits (bp_be/bp_be12, swerv_wrapper/swerv_wrapper43, black_parrot/bp_fe), which the authors acknowledge. The two genuinely unseen-family designs (ariane133, ariane136) are where the method struggles most: Refine alone achieves 2.15x WNS on ariane133, and even CoOpt+Refine yields 0.85x/0.42x and 0.84x/0.82x there. If you restrict the headline 0.78x/ 0.49x average to (ariane133+ariane136) alone, the picture is much closer to the prior SOTA (and on those two, TNS averages slightly above 1.0). The 22%/51% number is real but partially driven by family-similar test circuits imo.
The PPA framing is partly misleading. The predictor is trained for WNS+TNS+Power+Area but Sec. 5.3.2 notes "Power varies by less than 2% across configurations of the same circuit, so timing dominates the ranking signal," and area is fixed by floorplan. The method imo is a timing-driven surrogate that does not hurt power.
The paper claims Section 6.4 validates the fidelity finding across placers but Section 6.4 is about surrogate ranking accuracy, not about flow-stage correlations. The label-fidelity study has a small-sample issue that should also be acknowledged. The point estimates are reported without intervals, and the 20 configurations are systematic sweeps rather than samples from the placement-quality distribution.
Comments for authors
This is a well-written paper and a clear advance over the cross-stage prediction literature. The label-fidelity study is a very useful empirical finding, and it is the right anchor for the rest of the work.

The most important thing to address is the test-set lineage. Three of the five test circuits have same-family siblings in training, and the two that do not (ariane133, ariane136) are exactly where the method gains the least and where Refine alone actually loses. Please report the "lineage-stratified" averages explicitly (e.g., "same-family vs. truly-unseen" rows in Table 2) so the reader can see what the main results depends on.

The cross-placer narrative would also benefit from one more experiment. If you train the surrogate on a mix of DREAMPlace and RTLMP placements (or fine-tune the DREAMPlace-trained predictor on a small RTLMP set) and re-run the cross-placer evaluation in Table 4, the resulting numbers would tell us how much of the 0.77 vs 0.61 gap is fixable with mixed-source training. Given that Refine is claimed as placer-agnostic, this is worth showing.

For the label-fidelity study, please add confidence intervals on the Spearman rho values in Table 1 (point estimates are misleading in this case), and maybe (in future) consider re-running the fidelity check on DREAMPlace-generated placements as well as RTLMP. The finding will hopefully hold but the paper will be stronger if both placement distributions are covered.

Imo, please soften the "PPA" framing, given that power varies by <2% in training and area is fixed.

Rebuttal questions for authors
Three of five test circuits have same-family siblings in training. Please provide the Table 2 averages stratified by lineage: (a) same-family test circuits (swerv_wrapper, black_parrot, bp_be) vs. (b) truly unseen-family (ariane133, ariane136). What does the 22%/51% improvements reduce to on group (b) alone?
