\begin{abstract}
Macro placement significantly determines the chip's post-route performance, power, and area (PPA). Most chip placers set the half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL) as their major optimization objectives. However, recent benchmarking shows a near-zero correlation between HPWL and post-route timing metrics, and all evaluated AI placers degrade PPA relative to hierarchical baselines. Recent efforts, such as LaMPlace, aim to close this gap by training cross-stage predictors. But they rely on macro-centric representations, and the training data still comes from the pre-routing stage. This paper presents PPAPlace, a placer-agnostic framework that directly predicts post-route PPA from the full mixed-size placement stage. To find reliable training labels, a systematic study is conducted across design flow stages. The results show that post-global-routing metrics best reflect the final post-route PPA while remaining cheap to generate. In contrast, pre-route metrics can misrepresent the true PPA outcome. A dual-stream predictor combining graph attention over the netlist with spatial convolution over the placement grid is trained on these labels. Because the predictor takes any finished placement as input, it is not tied to a specific placement algorithm. Evaluated on five unseen ChiPBench circuits through a full RTL-to-signoff flow, PPAPlace improves TNS by 33\% and WNS by 18\% over Hier-RTLMP, outperforming LaMPlace by 13 and 7 percentage points respectively. All code and data will be publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{chip placement, PPA prediction, half-perimeter wirelength, design space exploration, graph attention network, physical design}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chip placement determines the physical locations of circuit modules on a
two-dimensional canvas and is one of the most significant steps in
VLSI physical design. The quality of placement directly affects the
chip's final performance, power consumption, and area (PPA), which
together determine whether a design meets its specifications and can be
manufactured~\cite{kahng2022vlsi}.

Existing placement approaches fall into two broad categories: analytical and AI-based placers. Analytical
placers such as DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} formulate placement
as continuous optimization of a smooth wirelength approximation plus
density penalties, solved by gradient descent on GPU. AI-based methods
including reinforcement
learning~\cite{mirhoseini2021graph, lai2022maskplace, lai2023chipformer},
black-box optimization~\cite{shi2023wire}, and diffusion
models~\cite{lee2024chip} replace or augment the optimizer with learned
policies. Despite their different approaches, most methods from both categories focus only on optimizing the half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL), a fast estimate of total wire length, as the primary placement objective.

However, growing evidence suggests that HPWL is a poor proxy for the metrics that
matter in practice. The ChiPBench benchmark~\cite{chipbench2025} evaluated six AI-based placement methods on 20 circuits through the full
OpenROAD~\cite{kahng2022openroad} chip design flow. ChiPBench uses
Hier-RTLMP, OpenROAD's built-in hierarchical macro placer, as the
baseline placement method. Hier-RTLMP leverages RTL design hierarchy and dataflow
structure rather than optimizing wirelength alone, yet every evaluated AI
method degraded PPA relative to it. ChiPBench further reported a Pearson
correlation of only $-0.08$ between macro HPWL and the worst negative slack
(WNS), confirming that wirelength optimization is essentially
uninformative for timing.

Recent efforts have begun to address this disconnect from both industry
and academia. Commercial tools such as Synopsys DSO.ai~\cite{dsoai2025} and Cadence Cerebrus~\cite{cerebrus2025} use reinforcement learning to tune tool configurations on top of existing analytical engines, treating placement as a design-space exploration problem. LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} trains a cross-stage predictor on
offline placement data and uses it to guide macro placement through
evolutionary search, achieving notable improvements in timing metrics.
These efforts demonstrate that learned PPA prediction can meaningfully
improve placement quality. However, two open questions remain for the
field: What is the most reliable source of supervision labels for cross-stage
prediction, and how much of the placement state does the predictor need
to observe.

This paper investigates both questions. To address the first, a systematic study is conducted across the chip design flow stages to determine
which stage provides training labels that best reflect post-route
outcomes. The results reveal that pre-route timing evaluation can be
misleading: configurations that appear timing-clean before routing
violate timing afterward, and their ranking reverses.
Post-global-routing evaluation, by contrast, better preserves the
post-route ranking in our experiments at roughly one-sixth the cost of
full routing. This finding has implications for any learning-based
approach that relies on cross-stage supervision.

To address the second question, a dual-stream predictor is proposed that
observes the complete mixed-size placement state rather than macro
positions alone. A graph attention network processes the netlist
connectivity, and a spatial convolutional network processes the
placement grid, capturing standard cell density, pin distribution, and
routing congestion estimates. Because the predictor takes any finished
placement as input regardless of how it was produced, it is not tied to
a specific placement algorithm. The paper demonstrates its application
through Bayesian optimization over DREAMPlace's hyperparameter space,
but the same predictor could guide configuration search for any
placement method.

The contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A label fidelity study across design flow stages. Pre-route
    PPA metrics are unreliable for ranking placement quality, while
    post-global-routing metrics offer the best trade-off between
    correlation with final outcomes and evaluation cost.

    \item A PPA predictor over the mixed-size placement state. Unlike
    macro-centric predictors, it captures standard cell density, pin
    distribution, and routing congestion through a dual-stream
    architecture combining graph attention with spatial convolution.

    \item A placer-agnostic optimization framework. The predictor
    scores any finished placement, enabling candidate selection or
    configuration search across different placement paradigms.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Analytical Placement}

Analytical placement has been the backbone of chip placement for decades. Among them, DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} reformulated analytical placement as a neural network training problem, enabling GPU acceleration with over 30$\times$ speedup compared to the CPU-based RePlAce~\cite{cheng2019replace}. Later versions added timing-driven net weighting~\cite{dreamplace4} and support for mixed-size macros. Building on DREAMPlace, AutoDMP~\cite{autodmp2023} introduced Bayesian optimization to automatically tune placement hyperparameters, an approach conceptually similar to what commercial tools like Synopsys DSO.ai~\cite{dsoai2025} do at a larger scale. DG-RePlAce~\cite{dgreplace2024} showed that exploiting dataflow structure in machine learning accelerators can reduce wirelength by 34\%. DREAMPlace~4.0~\cite{dreamplace4} introduced momentum-based net
weighting driven by static timing analysis, enabling the analytical
engine to prioritize timing-critical nets. While this reduces the
HPWL-to-timing gap within the placer, the net weights are derived from
pre-route STA estimates whose fidelity we question in
Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}. A common limitation across all these
methods is that they optimize HPWL or its timing-weighted variants,
sharing the HPWL-to-PPA gap that we aim to address.

\subsection{AI-Based Placement}

On the other hand, AI-based methods have explored fundamentally different
placement strategies. Despite the controversial reproducibility, AlphaChip~\cite{mirhoseini2021graph} pioneered deep reinforcement learning for macro placement~\cite{kahng2023reevaluating, markov2024falsedawn}. MaskPlace~\cite{lai2022maskplace} treated placement as visual representation learning, and ChiPFormer~\cite{lai2023chipformer} improved sample efficiency through offline RL. WireMask-BBO~\cite{shi2023wire} showed that evolutionary algorithms with appropriate problem formulations can match or outperform RL methods on wirelength. MaskRegulate~\cite{maskregulate2024} shifted the paradigm from placing macros from scratch to refining existing placements, and EfficientPlace~\cite{efficientplace2024} combined tree search with RL to
reduce exploration cost. Despite the diversity of these approaches, they
all optimize HPWL or macro HPWL as the primary objective.

\subsection{Cross-Stage PPA Prediction and Optimization}
LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} is a representative recent work that
directly targets the HPWL-to-PPA gap. It trains a Laurent polynomial
predictor on offline data to estimate cross-stage metrics from macro
pairwise distances and uses the predictor to generate a guidance mask
for WireMask-EA's evolutionary search, achieving 43\% and 30\%
improvements in TNS and WNS respectively. Its training labels are
obtained through DREAMPlace cell placement followed by OpenTimer static
timing analysis, providing pre-route timing supervision.
MacroRank~\cite{macrorank2023} trained a model to rank macro placements
by routing quality but did not integrate it into a placement optimizer.
PreRoutGNN~\cite{preroutgnn2024} predicted pre-routing timing with high
accuracy but focused on prediction rather than using the predictions to
improve placement. Re2MaP~\cite{re2map2024} learns to refine an initial macro placement by
remapping positions to improve PPA, and BBOPlace-Bench~\cite{bboplacebench2024}
provides a unified benchmark for black-box optimization approaches to
macro placement. Both highlight the importance of aligning the search
objective with downstream PPA rather than HPWL.
In the commercial space, Synopsys
DSO.ai~\cite{dsoai2025} and Cadence Cerebrus~\cite{cerebrus2025} use
reinforcement learning to tune tool configuration parameters on top of
existing analytical engines, treating placement as a design-space
exploration problem rather than modifying the placement objective itself.

\subsection{Benchmarking}

Traditional placement benchmarks such as ISPD 2005 and ICCAD 2004 evaluate
methods using wirelength and density metrics alone, without considering
post-route PPA. ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} addressed this limitation
by providing 20 circuits across CPUs, GPUs, and NPUs with a complete
OpenROAD-based evaluation flow that measures post-route WNS, TNS, power,
area, and DRC violations. The finding that every evaluated AI method
degrades PPA relative to the hierarchical baseline directly motivates our
work. We adopt ChiPBench's evaluation protocol as our primary benchmark.

\subsection{Chip Placement and Evaluation Metrics}

Chip placement assigns physical positions to circuit modules on a
two-dimensional canvas. A netlist hypergraph $H = (V, E)$ specifies the
modules $V$ (macros and standard cells) and the nets $E$ connecting
them. Placement methods seek positions
$\mathbf{x} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ that minimize a placement
objective. In analytical placers such as
DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace}, this takes the form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:placement}
\min_{\mathbf{x}} \; \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})
= \mathcal{W}(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}$ is a smooth wirelength approximation and
$\mathcal{D}$ is a density penalty that discourages cell
overlap~\cite{lu2015eplace}. The density weight $\lambda$ increases
iteratively to enforce legality. The wirelength term is typically based
on HPWL:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hpwl}
\mathrm{HPWL}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{e \in E}
\left[\max_{i \in e} x_i - \min_{i \in e} x_i
+ \max_{i \in e} y_i - \min_{i \in e} y_i\right]
\end{equation}
which estimates total wire length by summing the bounding-box
half-perimeters across all nets. HPWL is differentiable (via smooth
approximations such as the weighted-average
model~\cite{hsu2013wa}), decomposable across nets, and fast to compute.

However, the actual quality of a placed design is determined by
post-route PPA metrics. Given a placement $\mathbf{x}$, let
$\mathrm{Flow}(\mathbf{x})$ denote the result of executing the full
downstream design flow (cell placement, CTS, routing, and timing
analysis). The PPA outcome is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ppa}
\mathbf{y} = \mathrm{Flow}(\mathbf{x})
= \left[\mathrm{WNS},\; \mathrm{TNS},\;
\mathrm{Power},\; \mathrm{Area}\right]
\end{equation}
where WNS is the worst negative slack (the most critical timing
violation), TNS is the total negative slack (sum of all violations),
and power and area are the total power consumption and physical
footprint. Computing $\mathbf{y}$ requires running the full design flow,
which typically takes tens of minutes to hours per design.

\subsection{Design Flow Stages}

The design flow between placement and signoff consists of several
stages, each providing increasingly accurate but more expensive
evaluation. Let $\mathrm{Flow}_s(\mathbf{x})$ denote a partial flow
truncated at stage $s$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:partial_flow}
\mathbf{y}_s = \mathrm{Flow}_s(\mathbf{x}), \quad
s \in \{\text{CTS},\; \text{GRT},\; \text{DRT}\}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CTS} ($\mathbf{y}_{\text{CTS}}$): constructs a
    balanced clock distribution network that delivers the clock signal
    to all sequential elements, inserting buffers to meet setup timing
    constraints.
    \item \textbf{GRT} ($\mathbf{y}_{\text{GRT}}$): assigns nets to
    coarse routing regions across the chip, introducing
    congestion-dependent wire delay estimates.
    \item \textbf{DRT} ($\mathbf{y}_{\text{DRT}}$): assigns exact metal
    tracks and vias for every net, producing signoff-quality timing,
    power, and area metrics.
\end{itemize}

The goal of a cross-stage PPA predictor is to learn
$f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \approx \mathbf{y}_{\text{DRT}}$ from
placement-stage features, avoiding the cost of running
$\mathrm{Flow}(\mathbf{x})$ at inference time. A prerequisite is that
the training labels $\mathbf{y}_s$ used to supervise $f_\theta$ must
rank placements consistently with the final outcome
$\mathbf{y}_{\text{DRT}}$.
Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity} investigates which stage $s$ best
satisfies this requirement.

\section{Label Fidelity Analysis}
\label{sec:label_fidelity}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{graphs/ppaplace_overview.pdf}
    \caption{PPAPlace framework. (a)~Offline: diverse placement

    post-global-routing. A dual-stream predictor (GAT + CNN
    $\rightarrow$ MLP) is trained on the resulting (placement, PPA)

    \label{fig:overview}

Any learning-based PPA predictor requires training labels that rank
placements consistently with their final post-route quality. This
section investigates which stage offers the best trade-off between
ranking fidelity and evaluation cost across a diverse set of circuits.

\subsection{Setup}

The study spans 10 ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} circuits covering
RISC-V CPUs from three families (bp\_fe, bp\_be, and bp\_be12 from
BlackParrot, swerv\_wrapper from SweRV, ariane133 from Ariane), an
OpenRISC CPU (mor1kx), a neural processing unit (isa\_npu), and
peripheral designs (ethernet, dft68, vga\_lcd). bp\_be12 is a variant
of the BlackParrot backend at a coarser macro granularity (12 macros). Macro counts range from
10 to 132 and cell counts from 33K to 427K, ensuring that the findings
are not specific to a single design scale or application domain.

To generate diverse placements for each circuit, the weight parameters
of OpenROAD's RTLMP hierarchical macro
placer~\cite{kahng2022openroad} are systematically varied. RTLMP
optimizes a weighted combination of area utilization, wirelength,
boundary proximity, outline conformance, notch avoidance, and dead
space. By sweeping these weights, the same deterministic placer produces
structurally different macro arrangements while remaining within the
space of physically reasonable placements. For each circuit, 20
configurations are generated, including single-weight extremes (e.g.,
high or low area weight), pairwise combinations (e.g., high wirelength
and boundary weights), and globally scaled settings.

Each configuration is evaluated through the complete ChiPBench flow:
synthesis, floorplanning with the specified RTLMP weights, standard cell
placement, CTS, GRT, and DRT. PPA metrics (WNS, TNS, power) are
recorded at each stage. Area is excluded as it is determined by the
floorplan and does not vary across configurations of the same circuit.
Because the entire flow runs within a single ChiPBench pipeline, all
stages share the same synthesized netlist and extracted parasitics,
ensuring internal consistency across stages. Post-DRT metrics serve as
the ground truth throughout this analysis.

\subsection{Stage-Wise Rank Correlation}

Table~\ref{tab:rank_corr} reports the Spearman $\rho$ between WNS at
each intermediate stage and the post-route ground truth, computed over
the same set of placements per circuit.

Circuits are ordered by macro count (10 to 132). HPWL shows near-zero
correlation with post-route WNS across all circuits ($|\rho| < 0.22$),
confirming its unreliability as a timing proxy. Post-CTS correlation is
inconsistent: strongly positive for some circuits (bp\_be: $\rho =
0.83$) but negative for others (ethernet: $\rho = -0.31$; swerv:
$\rho = -0.12$). Post-GRT achieves consistently high correlation across
all 10 circuits ($\rho \geq 0.78$), with no circuit showing the sign
reversals observed at earlier stages. The pattern holds from the
smallest design (bp\_be, 10 macros, $\rho = 0.80$) to the largest
(ariane133, 132 macros, $\rho = 0.94$). GRT evaluation costs roughly
one-sixth of full DRT across all circuits tested.

\subsection{Implications}

The consistent pattern across 10 circuits spanning 10 to 132 macros,
33K to 427K cells, and five design categories (CPUs, NPU,
networking, DFT, display) demonstrates that post-global-routing is the
most cost-effective label stage for cross-stage PPA prediction.
Post-GRT evaluation achieves consistently high rank correlation with
post-route outcomes at roughly one-sixth the runtime of full detailed
routing. Labels from stages before global routing, including the
pre-route static timing used by existing
methods~\cite{lamplace2025}, fall in the range where ranking
disagreement with post-route outcomes is observed. While the severity
may vary across technology nodes and design styles, these findings
suggest that validation against post-route outcomes is necessary when
choosing a label source for cross-stage prediction.

\section{PPAPlace}
\label{sec:method}

Based on the findings in Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}, PPAPlace
uses post-global-routing labels as training supervision. The framework
consists of a PPA predictor trained on the mixed-size placement state
(Sections~\ref{subsec:representation}--\ref{subsec:training}) and a
placer-agnostic configuration optimization mechanism that uses the
predictor to guide any placement method
(Section~\ref{subsec:config}). Figure~\ref{fig:overview} illustrates
the overall framework.


\subsection{Mixed-Size Placement Representation}
\label{subsec:representation}

Existing cross-stage predictors such as LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} represent the
placement state through macro pairwise distances, discarding information
about standard cells, spatial density, and routing congestion. However,
timing violations and power consumption are largely determined by
standard cell placement and routing, not macro positions
alone~\cite{chipbench2025}. PPAPlace addresses this by observing the
complete mixed-size placement through two complementary representations.

\textbf{Spatial representation.} The placement canvas is rasterized into
a $64 \times 64$ grid with five channels:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Cell density: total cell area in each bin normalized by bin
    capacity.
    \item Pin density: number of I/O pins per bin.
    \item Macro occupancy: binary mask indicating macro presence.
    \item RUDY congestion: estimated routing demand per
    bin~\cite{rudy}, computed as $\sum_{e} 1/(W_e \cdot H_e)$ over
    nets $e$ whose bounding box overlaps the bin, where $W_e$ and $H_e$
    are bounding box dimensions.
    \item Net bounding box density: number of net bounding boxes
    overlapping each bin, capturing routing pressure from nets whose
    pins lie outside the bin.
\end{enumerate}
All channels are normalized to $[0, 1]$. This grid-based representation
captures spatial distribution patterns invisible to macro-only
predictors, including congestion hotspots and density imbalances.

\textbf{Graph representation.} The netlist is represented as a graph
$G = (V_G, E_G)$ where nodes correspond to macros. Edges are derived
from the netlist hypergraph: each multi-pin net connecting $k$ macros
produces $\binom{k}{2}$ undirected edges (clique expansion), with
duplicate edges merged and edge weights set to the number of shared
nets between each macro pair. Each node carries
a feature vector:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:node_features}
\mathbf{h}_i = [x_i,\; y_i,\; w_i,\; h_i,\; r_i,\; p_i,\;
n_i,\; s_i]
\end{equation}
where $(x_i, y_i)$ is normalized position, $(w_i, h_i)$ is normalized
width and height, $r_i = w_i / h_i$ is aspect ratio, $p_i$ is pin
count, $n_i$ is net degree (number of nets connected to this node), and
$s_i$ is average net span (mean HPWL of connected nets). The spatial
and graph streams observe strictly complementary information: the CNN
captures where density and congestion concentrate across the chip, while
the GAT captures what each module is and how far its connections reach.

Together, the spatial and graph representations provide the predictor
with both the global density landscape and the local connectivity
structure of the placement.


\subsection{Dual-Stream PPA Predictor}
\label{subsec:architecture}

The two representations are processed by separate encoder streams and
fused for prediction.

\textbf{Graph stream.} A 4-layer graph attention network
(GAT)~\cite{gat2018} with 128-dimensional hidden states and 4
attention heads process the netlist graph. Multi-head attention allows
each layer to learn different types of relationships between connected
nodes, such as spatial proximity and connectivity strength. Global Average
Pooling (GAP) aggregates all node embeddings into a fixed-size graph embedding
$\mathbf{e}_g \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$, regardless of circuit size.

\textbf{Spatial stream.} A lightweight CNN consisting of 3 convolutional
layers with ReLU activations progressively reduces the spatial
resolution of the $64 \times 64$ grid. GAP produces
a spatial embedding $\mathbf{e}_s \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$.

\textbf{Fusion and prediction.} The two embeddings are concatenated and
mapped to PPA predictions through a 2-layer MLP:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:predictor}
f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) =
\mathrm{MLP}\!\left([\mathbf{e}_g(\mathbf{x});\;
\mathbf{e}_s(\mathbf{x})]\right) \rightarrow
[\widehat{\mathrm{WNS}},\; \widehat{\mathrm{TNS}},\;
\widehat{\mathrm{Power}},\; \widehat{\mathrm{Area}}]
\end{equation}
The hat notation denotes predicted values. The architecture produces
fixed-size embeddings (512 dimensions total) regardless of circuit size,
enabling the same trained model to be applied across circuits with
different numbers of cells and macros.


\subsection{Training}
\label{subsec:training}

\subsubsection{Data Generation}

Training data is generated by running a placement method with $M$
randomized configurations per circuit. Each configuration varies
parameters that affect placement quality, such as target density and
density weight schedule. Each resulting placement is
evaluated through the OpenROAD flow to post-global-routing, producing
labels $\mathbf{y}_i = [\mathrm{WNS},\; \mathrm{TNS},\;
\mathrm{Power},\; \mathrm{Area}]$. The training set is
$\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{C \times M}$
across $C$ circuits.

\subsubsection{Loss Function}

The predictor is trained with a composite loss:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MSE}}
+ \lambda_r \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}}
\end{equation}
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MSE}}$ is the mean squared error between
predicted and true PPA values. $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}}$ is a
pairwise ranking loss over placement pairs from the same circuit:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ranking}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}} = \sum_{(i,j)}
\max\!\Big(0,\; -(\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{y}_j) \cdot
\big(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i) - f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_j)\big)\Big)
\end{equation}
where the sum is over all placement pairs $(i, j)$ from the same
circuit. All four PPA metrics are sign-unified so that lower values
indicate better quality (WNS and TNS are negated), and z-score
normalization per circuit ensures comparable scales across metrics.
The dot product is positive when the predicted ranking agrees with the
ground truth across all metrics simultaneously; the hinge
$\max(0, \cdot)$ incurs zero loss for correctly ranked pairs and a
linear penalty for misranked ones. With $M = 500$ samples per circuit,
the $\binom{500}{2} \approx 125$K pairs are computed exhaustively
without subsampling, as this remains tractable on modern GPUs.

MSE provides an absolute training signal that stabilizes learning,
while the ranking loss specifically optimizes ordinal accuracy.
For downstream configuration selection
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:select}), the argmin operates over the predicted
WNS component of $f_\theta$, selecting the placement with the least
predicted timing violation.

\subsubsection{Cross-Circuit Generalization}

Three design choices are made to enable the predictor to generalize across circuits without retraining. First, the $64 \times 64$ spatial grid normalizes
all circuits to the same resolution regardless of actual canvas dimensions. Second, global pooling in both streams produces fixed-size embeddings independent of the number of nodes. Third, labels are z-score normalized per circuit, forcing the predictor to learn relative quality patterns rather than circuit-specific absolute values. These patterns, such as the relationship between density uniformity and timing quality, transfer across circuits because they reflect physical principles common to all designs.


\subsection{Placer-Agnostic Configuration Optimization}
\label{subsec:config}

The trained predictor $f_\theta$ provides a fast approximation of
post-route PPA without running the downstream flow. PPAPlace exploits
this by searching over a placer's configuration space and selecting the
configuration whose resulting placement receives the best predicted PPA,
without modifying the placer's internals.

Let $\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{h})$ denote a placement method with
configuration $\mathbf{h}$. The objective is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:config}
\mathbf{h}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{h} \in \mathcal{H}} \;
f_\theta\!\big(\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{h})\big)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}$ is the configuration space. Because $f_\theta$
takes a finished placement as input rather than any placer-specific
intermediate state, Eq.~\eqref{eq:config} applies to any method that
can produce candidate placements. For analytical placers such as
DREAMPlace, $\mathbf{h}$ includes target density, density weight, and
random seed. For evolutionary methods, $\mathbf{h}$ may include
population size and mutation parameters. For RL or diffusion-based
methods, different rollout seeds serve as the configuration variable.
This paper demonstrates the framework on DREAMPlace; evaluation on
other placement paradigms is left for future work.

Two strategies are natural. When the configuration space is continuous,
Bayesian optimization over $f_\theta \circ \mathcal{P}$ can
iteratively select promising configurations without running the
downstream flow. When the candidate pool is finite, a simpler
enumerate-and-select strategy applies. Given $K$ pre-computed
placements from any source, the predictor ranks them and selects the
best:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:select}
\mathbf{x}^* = \arg\min_{k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}} \;
f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_k)
\end{equation}
Only the selected placement proceeds through the full post-route flow,
reducing the number of expensive evaluations from $K$ to one.

\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:setup}

\textbf{Benchmarks.} Experiments are conducted on
ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} circuits synthesized with the Nangate45
open cell library. Ten circuits are used for training (bp\_fe, bp\_be,
bp\_be12, isa\_npu, swerv\_wrapper, vga\_lcd, ethernet, dft68, mor1kx,
ariane133); five circuits not seen during training are used for
end-to-end evaluation (VeriGPU, ariane136, black\_parrot, or1200,
swerv\_wrapper43). Within the training set, an 80/20 within-circuit
split is used for predictor ranking evaluation, and leave-one-circuit-out
(LOCO) cross-validation tests cross-circuit generalization
(Section~\ref{subsec:generalization}). All placements are evaluated
through the full OpenROAD flow: synthesis $\rightarrow$ floorplanning
$\rightarrow$ placement $\rightarrow$ CTS $\rightarrow$ GRT
$\rightarrow$ DRT $\rightarrow$ timing analysis, yielding post-route
WNS, TNS, power, and area.

\textbf{Training data.} For each training circuit, $M = 500$ diverse
placements are generated by varying DREAMPlace mixed-size placer
hyperparameters (target density, density weight, learning rate, and
random seed) and evaluated through the OpenROAD flow to post-GRT,
producing approximately 5{,}000 (placement, PPA) training pairs across
10 circuits. For the label stage comparison experiments
(Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}), a separate set of RTLMP weight
configurations per circuit is additionally evaluated to post-CTS,
post-placement STA, and post-DRT.

\textbf{Baselines.}
Six baselines are compared.
\emph{Hier-RTLMP} is OpenROAD's default hierarchical macro placer and serves as the ChiPBench baseline (normalized to 1.00).
\emph{DREAMPlace}~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} uses unmodified analytical placement with default configuration.
\emph{AutoDMP}~\cite{autodmp2023} applies Bayesian hyperparameter optimization to DREAMPlace, evaluated by HPWL.
\emph{LaMPlace}~\cite{lamplace2025} learns a cross-stage mask with WireMask-EA; we report their published ChiPBench results from Appendix~B.1, marked with $^\dagger$ in tables, as direct rerun was not feasible within the submission timeline.
Two additional selection baselines operate on the same $K$-candidate pool as PPAPlace: \emph{HPWL-select} picks the configuration with the lowest HPWL, and \emph{Random-select} picks one uniformly at random.

\textbf{Metrics.} Post-route WNS (ps), TNS (ns), power (mW), and area
($\mu$m$^2$). All values normalized by the Hier-RTLMP baseline (lower
is better). Runtime reported as wall-clock time.

\textbf{Training details.} All predictors are trained with Adam
(lr = $5 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$) for 200 epochs with
batch size 32. The ranking loss weight is $\lambda_r = 0.1$. Within
each circuit's samples, batches are formed by same-circuit grouping to
ensure ranking pairs share the same netlist. We report mean $\pm$ std
over 3 random seeds unless otherwise noted.

\subsection{Cross-Circuit Generalization}
\label{subsec:generalization}

To test whether the predictor transfers to entirely unseen circuits,
leave-one-circuit-out (LOCO) cross-validation is performed: in each of
10 folds, the predictor is trained on 9 circuits and evaluated on the
held-out circuit's configurations. Figure~\ref{fig:results}(c)
reports per-circuit ranking accuracy when that circuit is the held-out
fold.

Ranking accuracy varies across circuits, with $\tau$ ranging from
$0.11 \pm 0.02$ on ethernet to $0.24 \pm 0.03$ on ariane133 (mean
$\pm$ std over 3 seeds). Circuits whose design families are well
represented in the training set (e.g., RISC-V CPUs) tend to show higher
transfer accuracy, while circuits with more distinct characteristics
show lower but still positive $\tau$.

While these $\tau$ values are moderate in absolute terms, the
downstream task is top-$k$ selection rather than full ranking. Across
all 10 folds, the LOCO predictor selects a top-10\% placement (from 500
candidates) 68\% of the time, compared to 10\% expected by chance. This
confirms that even modest ranking correlation translates to practical
selection utility. The overall positive LOCO accuracy across all 10
folds suggests that the predictor captures transferable physical
patterns, though the magnitude of transfer would likely improve with
more diverse training circuits.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented PPAPlace, a framework for enhancing chip placement
through mixed-size PPA prediction. The central finding is that
cross-stage placement learning depends critically on two factors that
prior work has not jointly examined: supervision fidelity and
representation completeness.

A controlled study across 10 circuits shows that post-global-routing
labels achieve $\rho \geq 0.78$ with post-route outcomes, while
post-CTS labels are inconsistent and HPWL is uninformative. A $2 \times
2$ factorial experiment confirms that both label fidelity and
representation completeness contribute independently to ranking
accuracy. On five circuits unseen during training, PPAPlace improves
WNS by 18\% and TNS by 33\% over the Hier-RTLMP hierarchical baseline,
outperforming the next-best method (LaMPlace) on all circuits.

\textbf{Limitations.} The predictor requires offline training data
generation through the design flow to post-GRT. The current evaluation
is limited to DREAMPlace configuration selection on ChiPBench circuits with
the Nangate45 library; generalization to other technology nodes and
design styles requires further validation. Published LaMPlace results
are used where direct rerun was not feasible, limiting the comparison to
reported numbers rather than fully controlled experiments.

\textbf{Future directions.} Replacing enumerate-and-select with Bayesian
optimization over the DREAMPlace configuration space could improve
sample efficiency by focusing evaluations on promising regions.
Extending the framework to AI-based placement methods (e.g., RL and
diffusion models) would test the generality of the placer-agnostic
approach. Investigating whether the predictor's gradients with respect
to cell positions provide useful optimization signals for direct
integration into analytical placement loops is a longer-term direction,
contingent on validating gradient fidelity against downstream outcomes.
