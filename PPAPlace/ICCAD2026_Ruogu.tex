\begin{abstract}
Macro placement significantly determines the chip's post-route performance, power, and area (PPA). Most chip placers optimize the half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL), yet recent benchmarking shows a near-zero correlation between HPWL and post-route timing metrics, and all evaluated AI placers degrade PPA relative to hierarchical baselines. Recent efforts such as LaMPlace train cross-stage predictors to close this gap, but rely on macro-centric representations with pre-routing supervision. This paper presents PPASurrogate, a framework that predicts post-route PPA from the full mixed-size placement state and uses the prediction as a surrogate objective for configuration optimization. A label fidelity study across 10 circuits and four design flow stages establishes post-global-routing as the most cost-effective supervision stage, while HPWL and pre-route timing are shown to be uninformative. A dual-stream predictor combining graph attention over the netlist with spatial convolution over the placement grid is trained on these post-GRT labels. Deployed through Bayesian optimization over a placer's hyperparameter space, PPASurrogate finds configurations that yield high-quality placements. On five unseen ChiPBench circuits, PPASurrogate achieves average WNS and TNS improvements of 12\% and 21\% over the hierarchical baseline in 50 trials, while all prior AI methods remain above the baseline. Because the predictor evaluates finished placements rather than placer-specific state, it transfers across placement paradigms without retraining.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{chip placement, PPA prediction, Bayesian optimization, surrogate-guided optimization, graph attention network, physical design}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chip placement determines the physical locations of circuit modules on a
two-dimensional canvas and is one of the most significant steps in
VLSI physical design. The quality of placement directly affects the
chip's final performance, power consumption, and area (PPA), which
together determine whether a design meets its specifications and can be
manufactured~\cite{kahng2022vlsi}.

Existing placement approaches fall into two broad categories: analytical and AI-based placers. Analytical
placers such as DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} formulate placement
as continuous optimization of a smooth wirelength approximation plus
density penalties, solved by gradient descent on GPU. AI-based methods
including reinforcement
learning~\cite{mirhoseini2021graph, lai2022maskplace, lai2023chipformer},
black-box optimization~\cite{shi2023wire}, and diffusion
models~\cite{lee2024chip} replace or augment the optimizer with learned
policies. Despite the methodological differences between these two categories, most methods focus only on optimizing the half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL), a fast estimate of total wire length, as the primary placement objective.

However, growing evidence suggests that HPWL is a poor proxy for the metrics that
matter in practice. The ChiPBench benchmark~\cite{chipbench2025} evaluated six AI-based placement methods on 20 circuits through the full
OpenROAD~\cite{ajayi2019openroad} chip design flow. ChiPBench uses
Hier-RTLMP (register-transfer level macro placer), OpenROAD's built-in hierarchical macro placer, as the
baseline placement method. Hier-RTLMP leverages RTL design hierarchy and dataflow
structure rather than optimizing wirelength alone, yet every evaluated AI
method degraded PPA relative to it. ChiPBench further reported a Pearson
correlation of only $-0.08$ between macro HPWL and the worst negative slack
(WNS), confirming that wirelength optimization is essentially
uninformative for timing.

Recent efforts have begun to address this disconnect from two
directions. AutoDMP~\cite{autodmp2023} tunes DREAMPlace's configuration
parameters through multi-objective Bayesian optimization, using
post-placement proxies (RSMT wirelength, cell density, and RUDY
congestion) to guide the search. While effective at improving placement
diversity, these proxies are computed before routing and inherit the
same proxy-to-PPA gap: the paper itself notes that proxy-optimal
placements do not always correspond to the best post-route PPA.
LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} takes a complementary approach, training a
cross-stage predictor on offline placement data and using it to guide
macro placement through evolutionary search, achieving notable timing
improvements. While these results confirm that cross-stage prediction
is a viable path to better placement, existing approaches share two
unexamined assumptions. First, they adopt pre-route timing from
placement-based static timing analysis (STA) as supervision data
without verifying that such labels faithfully preserve PPA rankings
after routing. Placement
STA estimates wire delays from coordinates alone and assumes ideal
clocks, omitting the physical effects introduced by clock tree
synthesis and routing. As we show in
Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}, placement STA labels exhibit
near-zero rank correlation with post-route timing ($\rho = 0.09$,
$p > 0.05$ on bp\_fe), making them no more informative than random
ordering for ranking placements. Second, they represent the placement
through macro positions alone, discarding the standard cell density
and routing congestion that ultimately govern timing and power.

This paper carefully evaluated both assumptions. A controlled study across 10 ChiPBench circuits reveals that pre-route timing labels, which were used as supervision data in prior work, can be misleading. Post-global-routing (GRT) labels, by contrast, consistently achieve high rank correlations with final sign-off PPA and remain inexpensive to generate. This finding establishes a principled basis for supervision stage selection in any cross-stage learning approach, whether for PPA prediction, placement optimization, or design-space exploration.

Building on this finding, PPASurrogate trains a dual-stream predictor on post-GRT labels over the complete mixed-size placement state, encoding
netlist connectivity via graph attention and spatial density via
convolutional analysis. Because the predictor evaluates the placement itself rather than any placer-specific state, the same trained surrogate readily applies to any existing or future placer with tunable configurations. PPASurrogate deploys this surrogate
through Bayesian optimization (BO) over the placer's configuration
space to find configurations that lead to high-quality placements.

The contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A label fidelity study across 10 circuits and four design stages, establishing post-global-routing as the most cost-effective supervision stage for cross-stage PPA prediction.

    \item A dual-stream predictor over the complete mixed-size placement state, achieving nearly $4\times$ higher ranking accuracy than the macro-only, pre-route paradigm.

    \item A surrogate-guided configuration optimization framework that, unlike AutoDMP's post-placement proxies, provides routing-aware feedback directly within the BO loop. PPASurrogate-BO improves WNS by 12\% and TNS by 21\% over the hierarchical baseline in 50 trials. Because the predictor evaluates finished placements rather than placer internals, the same trained model transfers across placement paradigms: trained on DREAMPlace data, it ranks RTLMP placements with $\rho = 0.63$ without retraining---a capability no prior configuration optimizer supports.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Analytical placement}
Analytical placers formulate placement as continuous optimization of a
smooth wirelength objective subject to density
constraints~\cite{lu2015eplace, cheng2019replace}.
DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} recast this formulation as a neural
network training problem, achieving over $30\times$ GPU speedup.
DREAMPlace~4.0~\cite{dreamplace4} added net weighting driven by static
timing analysis to prioritize timing-critical nets. However, these net
weights are derived from pre-route STA estimates whose fidelity we
question in Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}.
AutoDMP~\cite{autodmp2023} is the most closely related work: it tunes
16 DREAMPlace parameters via multi-objective TPE, using RSMT wirelength,
cell density, and RUDY congestion as BO objectives. These post-placement
proxies are fast to compute but, as AutoDMP acknowledges, do not always
correlate with post-route PPA. AutoDMP mitigates this through a
two-level scheme: the top Pareto-optimal candidates are re-evaluated in
a commercial EDA tool after routing. PPASurrogate addresses the same
proxy-to-PPA gap differently: instead of relying on post-placement proxies and
deferring accurate evaluation to a second level, it trains a surrogate
on post-global-routing labels (Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}) to provide
routing-aware feedback directly within the BO loop. This enables
single-level optimization with a quality-aware objective, amortized
training across circuits, and cross-placer transfer.
Commercial tools such
as Synopsys DSO.ai~\cite{dsoai2025} and Cadence
Cerebrus~\cite{cerebrus2025} apply reinforcement learning to tune tool
configurations at a larger scale.

\paragraph{AI-based placement}
AlphaChip~\cite{mirhoseini2021graph} pioneered deep reinforcement
learning for macro placement, though its reproducibility remains
debated~\cite{kahng2023reevaluating}. Subsequent work explored visual
representation learning~\cite{lai2022maskplace}, offline
RL~\cite{lai2023chipformer}, evolutionary
search~\cite{shi2023wire}, placement
refinement~\cite{maskregulate2024}, and tree-search-guided
RL~\cite{efficientplace2024}. Despite this diversity, all methods
optimize HPWL or macro HPWL as the primary objective.

\paragraph{Cross-stage PPA prediction and optimization}
LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} trains a Laurent polynomial predictor on
offline data to estimate cross-stage metrics from macro pairwise
distances. It uses the predictor to guide WireMask-EA's evolutionary
search, achieving notable timing improvements. Its training labels
come from DREAMPlace cell placement followed by OpenTimer static
timing analysis, a placement-based STA that estimates wire delays
from coordinates alone and assumes ideal clocks. As shown in
Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}, such pre-route labels can exhibit
near-zero rank correlation with post-route outcomes, raising the
question of whether the predictor optimizes a proxy that reflects
final PPA.
MacroRank~\cite{macrorank2023} and
PreRoutGNN~\cite{preroutgnn2024} predicted placement or routing quality
but did not use these predictions to improve placement.
ReMaP~\cite{re2map2025} and
BBOPlace-Bench~\cite{bboplacebench2025} highlight the importance of
aligning the search objective with downstream PPA rather than HPWL.
Across these efforts, a common gap remains: no work has systematically
validated which design flow stage provides the most reliable supervision
for cross-stage prediction.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Chip Placement and Evaluation Metrics}

Chip placement assigns physical positions to circuit modules on a
two-dimensional canvas. A netlist hypergraph $H = (V, E)$ specifies the
modules $V$ (macros and standard cells) and the nets $E$ connecting
them. Placement methods seek positions
$\mathbf{x} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ that minimize a placement
objective. In analytical placers such as
DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace}, this takes the form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:placement}
\min_{\mathbf{x}} \; \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})
= \mathcal{W}(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}$ is a smooth wirelength approximation and
$\mathcal{D}$ is a density penalty that discourages cell
overlap~\cite{lu2015eplace}. The density weight $\lambda$ increases
iteratively to enforce legality. The wirelength term is typically based
on HPWL:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hpwl}
\mathrm{HPWL}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{e \in E}
\left[\max_{i \in e} x_i - \min_{i \in e} x_i
+ \max_{i \in e} y_i - \min_{i \in e} y_i\right]
\end{equation}
which estimates total wire length by summing the bounding-box
half-perimeters across all nets. HPWL is differentiable (via smooth
approximations such as the weighted-average
model~\cite{hsu2013wa}), decomposable across nets, and fast to compute.

However, the actual quality of a placed design is determined by
post-route PPA metrics obtained after executing the downstream flow:
clock tree synthesis (CTS), global routing (GRT), and detailed routing
(DRT), each adding fidelity to timing and congestion estimates. Let
$\mathrm{Flow}(\mathbf{x})$ denote the full flow and
$\mathrm{Flow}_s(\mathbf{x})$ the flow truncated at stage $s$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ppa}
\mathbf{y} = \mathrm{Flow}(\mathbf{x})
= \left[\mathrm{WNS},\; \mathrm{TNS},\;
\mathrm{Power},\; \mathrm{Area}\right]
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:partial_flow}
\mathbf{y}_s = \mathrm{Flow}_s(\mathbf{x}), \quad
s \in \{\text{CTS},\; \text{GRT},\; \text{DRT}\}
\end{equation}
where WNS is the worst negative slack, TNS is the total negative slack,
and power and area are total power consumption and physical footprint.
Computing $\mathbf{y}$ typically takes tens of minutes to hours.

The goal of a cross-stage PPA predictor is to learn
$f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \approx \mathbf{y}_{\text{DRT}}$ from
placement-stage features, thus avoiding the cost of running
$\mathrm{Flow}(\mathbf{x})$ at inference time. A prerequisite is that
the training labels $\mathbf{y}_s$ used to supervise $f_\theta$ must
rank different quality of placements consistently with the final outcome
$\mathbf{y}_{\text{DRT}}$.
Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity} investigates which stage $s$ best
satisfies this requirement.

This section investigates which stage offers the best trade-off between placement quality
ranking fidelity and evaluation cost across a diverse set of circuits.

\subsection{Setup}

The study spans 10 ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} circuits covering
RISC-V CPUs from three families (bp\_fe, bp\_be, and bp\_be12 from
BlackParrot, swerv\_wrapper from SweRV, ariane133 from Ariane), an
OpenRISC CPU (mor1kx), a neural processing unit (isa\_npu), and
peripheral designs (ethernet, dft68, vga\_lcd). bp\_be12 is a variant
of the BlackParrot backend at a coarser macro granularity (12 macros).
Macro counts range from 10 to 132 and cell counts from 33K to 427K,
ensuring that the findings are not specific to a single design scale
or application domain. Note that this fidelity test uses RTLMP weight
configurations evaluated through the full flow, and is independent of
the training/test split used for the main experiments in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

To generate diverse placements for each circuit, the weight parameters
of OpenROAD's RTLMP hierarchical macro
placer~\cite{ajayi2019openroad} are systematically varied. RTLMP
optimizes a weighted combination of area utilization, wirelength,
boundary proximity, outline conformance, notch avoidance, and dead
space. By sweeping these weights, the placer produces
structurally different macro arrangements while remaining within the
space of physically reasonable placements. For each circuit, 20 different
configurations are generated, including single-weight extremes (e.g.,
high or low area weight), pairwise combinations (e.g., high wirelength
and boundary weights), and globally scaled settings.

Each configuration is evaluated through the complete ChiPBench flow:
synthesis, floorplanning with the specified RTLMP weights, standard cell
placement, CTS, GRT, and DRT. PPA metrics (WNS, TNS, power) are
recorded at each stage. Area is excluded because it is determined by the
floorplan and remains constant across different placements for the same circuit.
Because the entire flow runs within a single ChiPBench pipeline, all
stages share the same synthesized netlist and extracted parasitics,
ensuring internal consistency across stages. Post-DRT metrics serve as
the ground truth throughout this analysis.

\subsection{Stage-Wise Rank Correlation}

Table~\ref{tab:rank_corr} reports Spearman $\rho$ between WNS/TNS at
each intermediate stage and post-route ground truth. HPWL shows
near-zero correlation across all circuits ($|\rho| < 0.22$). Post-CTS
is inconsistent: strongly positive for some (bp\_be: $\rho = 0.83$) but
negative for others (ethernet: $\rho = -0.31$). Post-GRT achieves
$\rho \geq 0.78$ on all 10 circuits with no sign reversals, from the
smallest design (bp\_be, 10 macros) to the largest (ariane133, 132
macros), at roughly one-sixth the cost of full DRT. Pre-CTS placement
STA---the label source used by LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025}---fares no
better than HPWL: $|\rho| \leq 0.22$ on every circuit with no
statistically significant correlation. Post-GRT is thus the most
cost-effective supervision stage, while post-CTS labels show negative
$\rho$ on 3 of 10 circuits and can reverse rankings.

\section{PPASurrogate}
\label{sec:method}

Based on the findings in Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}, PPASurrogate
uses post-global-routing labels as training supervision. The framework
consists of a PPA predictor trained on the mixed-size placement state
(Sections~\ref{subsec:representation}--\ref{subsec:training}) and a
surrogate-guided configuration optimization mechanism. This framework supports
both candidate selection and Bayesian optimization, which uses the
predictor to guide any placement method (Section~\ref{subsec:config}). Figure~\ref{fig:overview} illustrates
the overall framework.


\subsection{Mixed-Size Placement Representation}
\label{subsec:representation}

Existing cross-stage predictors such as LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} represent the
placement state through macro pairwise distances, discarding information
about standard cells, spatial density, and routing congestion. However,
timing violations and power consumption are largely determined by
standard cell placement and routing, not macro positions
alone~\cite{chipbench2025}. PPASurrogate addresses this by observing the
complete mixed-size placement through two complementary representations.

\textbf{Spatial representation.} The placement canvas is rasterized into
a $64 \times 64$ grid with five channels:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Cell density: total cell area in each bin normalized by bin
    capacity.
    \item Pin density: number of I/O pins per bin.
    \item Macro occupancy: binary mask indicating macro presence.
    \item Rectangular Uniform wire DensitY (RUDY) congestion: estimated routing demand per
    bin~\cite{rudy}, computed as $\sum_{e} 1/(W_e \cdot H_e)$ over
    nets $e$ whose bounding box overlaps the bin, where $W_e$ and $H_e$
    are bounding box dimensions.
    \item Net bounding box density: number of net bounding boxes
    overlapping each bin, capturing routing pressure from nets whose
    pins lie outside the bin.
\end{enumerate}
All channels are normalized to $[0, 1]$. This grid-based representation
captures spatial distribution patterns invisible to macro-only
predictors, including congestion hotspots and density imbalances.

\textbf{Graph representation.} The netlist is represented as a graph
$G = (V_G, E_G)$ where nodes correspond to macros. Edges are derived
from the netlist hypergraph: each multi-pin net connecting $k$ macros
produces $\binom{k}{2}$ undirected edges (clique expansion), with
duplicate edges merged and edge weights set to the number of shared
nets between each macro pair. Each node carries
a feature vector:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:node_features}
\mathbf{h}_i = [x_i,\; y_i,\; w_i,\; h_i,\; r_i,\; p_i,\;
n_i,\; s_i]
\end{equation}
where $(x_i, y_i)$ is normalized position, $(w_i, h_i)$ is normalized
width and height, $r_i = w_i / h_i$ is aspect ratio, $p_i$ is pin
count, $n_i$ is net degree (number of nets connected to this node), and
$s_i$ is average net span (mean HPWL of connected nets). 

The spatial and graph streams observe strictly complementary information: the CNN
captures where density and congestion concentrate across the chip, while the GAT captures what each module is and how far its connections reach. Together, the spatial and graph representations provide the predictor
with both the global density landscape and the local connectivity
structure of the placement.

\subsection{Dual-Stream PPA Predictor}
\label{subsec:architecture}

The two representations are processed by separate encoder streams and
fused for prediction.

\textbf{Spatial stream.} A lightweight CNN consisting of 3 convolutional
layers with ReLU activations progressively reduces the spatial
resolution of the $64 \times 64$ grid. GAP produces
a spatial embedding $\mathbf{e}_s \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$.

\textbf{Graph stream.} A 4-layer graph attention network
(GAT)~\cite{gat2018} with 128-dimensional hidden states and 4
attention heads process the netlist graph. Multi-head attention allows
each layer to learn different types of relationships between connected
nodes, such as spatial proximity and connectivity strength. Global Average
Pooling (GAP) aggregates all node embeddings into a fixed-size graph embedding
$\mathbf{e}_g \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$, regardless of circuit size.

\textbf{Fusion and prediction.} The two embeddings are concatenated and
mapped to PPA predictions through a 2-layer MLP:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:predictor}
f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) =
\mathrm{MLP}\!\left([\mathbf{e}_g(\mathbf{x});\;
\mathbf{e}_s(\mathbf{x})]\right) \rightarrow
[\widehat{\mathrm{WNS}},\; \widehat{\mathrm{TNS}},\;
\widehat{\mathrm{Power}},\; \widehat{\mathrm{Area}}]
\end{equation}
The hat notation denotes predicted values. The architecture produces
fixed-size embeddings (512 dimensions total) regardless of circuit size,
enabling the same trained model to be applied across circuits with
different numbers of cells and macros.


\subsection{Training}
\label{subsec:training}

\subsubsection{Data Generation}

Training data is generated by running a placement method with $M$
randomized configurations per circuit. Each configuration varies
parameters that affect placement quality, such as target density and
density weight schedule. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}, each resulting placement is
evaluated through the OpenROAD flow to post-global-routing, producing
labels $\mathbf{y}_i = [\mathrm{WNS},\; \mathrm{TNS},\;
\mathrm{Power},\; \mathrm{Area}]$. The training set is
$\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{C \times M}$
across $C$ circuits.

\subsubsection{Loss Function}

The predictor is trained with a composite loss:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MSE}}
+ \lambda_r \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}}
\end{equation}
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MSE}}$ is the mean squared error between
predicted and true PPA values. The core task of the predictor is not to estimate exact PPA values,
but to correctly rank which placements are better and which are worse.
Because of this, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}}$ is a pairwise ranking loss
computed over placement pairs from the same circuit:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ranking}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}} = \sum_{(i,j)}
\max\!\Big(0,\; -(\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{y}_j) \cdot
\big(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i) - f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_j)\big)\Big)
\end{equation}
where the sum is over all placement pairs $(i, j)$ from the same
circuit. All four PPA metrics are sign-unified so that lower values
indicate better quality (WNS and TNS are negated), and z-score
normalization per circuit ensures comparable scales across metrics.
The dot product is positive when the predicted ordering agrees with
the ground truth across all metrics simultaneously; this implicitly
assumes co-monotonic improvements, which is benign in practice because
power varies by less than 2\% across configurations
(Table~\ref{tab:main}), so timing dominates the product. The hinge
$\max(0, \cdot)$ incurs zero loss for correctly ranked pairs and a
linear penalty for misranked ones. This formulation directly optimizes
what the downstream configuration optimization requires: given two
placements from the same circuit, the predictor should prefer the one
with better post-route PPA. With $M = 500$ samples per circuit, the
$\binom{500}{2} \approx 125$K pairs are computed exhaustively without
subsampling, as this remains tractable on modern GPUs.
Because area is determined by the floorplan and constant within each
circuit, the area component of $(\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{y}_j)$ is zero
for same-circuit pairs and contributes nothing to the ranking loss.
Area is retained in the MSE term as an auxiliary target that encourages
circuit-level feature learning across the shared architecture.

With this composite loss function, the MSE provides an absolute training signal that stabilizes learning,
while the ranking loss specifically optimizes ordinal accuracy.

\subsubsection{Cross-Circuit Generalization}

Three design choices are made to enable the predictor to generalize across circuits without retraining. First, the $64 \times 64$ spatial grid normalizes
all circuits to the same resolution regardless of actual canvas dimensions. Second, global pooling in both streams produces fixed-size embeddings independent of the number of nodes. Third, labels are z-score normalized per circuit, forcing the predictor to learn relative quality patterns rather than circuit-specific absolute values. These patterns, such as the relationship between density uniformity and timing quality, transfer across circuits because they reflect physical principles common to all designs.


\subsection{Placer-Agnostic Configuration Optimization}
\label{subsec:config}

The trained predictor $f_\theta$ provides a fast approximation of
post-route PPA without running the downstream flow. PPASurrogate exploits
this by searching over a placer's configuration space to find the
configuration that generates placement with the best predicted PPA. Let $\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{h})$ denote a placement method with
configuration $\mathbf{h}$. The objective is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:config}
\mathbf{h}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{h} \in \mathcal{H}} \;
f_\theta\!\big(\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{h})\big)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}$ is the configuration space. Because $f_\theta$
takes a finished placement as input rather than any placer-specific
intermediate state, Eq.~\eqref{eq:config} applies to any method that
can produce candidate placements. In both deployment modes below, the
same trained predictor serves as the objective function.

\subsubsection{Surrogate-Guided Placement Selection}
When a finite pool of $K$ candidate placements already exists, the
predictor ranks them and selects the best:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:select}
\mathbf{x}^* = \arg\min_{k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}} \;
f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_k)
\end{equation}
Only the selected placement proceeds through the full post-route flow,
reducing the number of expensive evaluations from $K$ to one. This
strategy is applicable when pre-computed candidates are available, for
example from design-space exploration or legacy runs.

\subsubsection{Surrogate-Guided Bayesian Optimization}
When the configuration space is continuous, Bayesian optimization (BO)
over the composite objective $f_\theta \circ \mathcal{P}$ can
iteratively propose promising configurations. At each trial $t$, the
BO sampler proposes $\mathbf{h}_t$, the placer produces
$\mathbf{x}_t = \mathcal{P}(\mathbf{h}_t)$, and the surrogate
evaluates $f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t)$ without running the downstream flow.
The sampler then updates its model and proposes the next trial.
Because TPE requires a scalar objective, the four-dimensional prediction
is scalarized as
$\mathrm{obj}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}) = \widehat{\mathrm{WNS}} +
\widehat{\mathrm{TNS}}$,
an equal-weight sum of the two timing metrics (both z-score normalized
so that lower is better). Power is excluded because it varies less than
2\% across configurations (Table~\ref{tab:main}), and area is excluded
because it is constant per circuit.

PPASurrogate uses the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator
(TPE)~\cite{bergstra2011tpe} as the BO sampler, which handles the
mixed parameter types (continuous, integer, categorical) naturally
present in placer configuration spaces. The random seed is fixed during
optimization to decouple configuration quality from seed-specific noise.
The best configuration is subsequently validated across multiple seeds.

After convergence, the top-$K$ configurations identified by BO are
validated through the full post-route flow to obtain ground-truth PPA.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:bo} summarizes the procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Surrogate-Guided Configuration Optimization}
\label{alg:bo}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Placer $\mathcal{P}$, predictor $f_\theta$, search space
$\mathcal{H}$, budget $T$, seed $s$
\STATE Initialize TPE sampler with $n_0{=}10$ random startup trials
\FOR{$t = 1, \ldots, T$}
  \STATE $\mathbf{h}_t \leftarrow$ TPE proposes configuration
  \STATE $\mathbf{x}_t \leftarrow \mathcal{P}(\mathbf{h}_t; s)$
  \hfill\COMMENT{run placer with fixed seed}
  \IF{placement failed}
    \STATE Mark trial as pruned; \textbf{continue}
  \ENDIF
  \STATE $\hat{\mathbf{y}}_t \leftarrow f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t)$
  \hfill\COMMENT{surrogate prediction ($<$0.1\,s)}
  \STATE $o_t \leftarrow \widehat{\mathrm{WNS}}_t + \widehat{\mathrm{TNS}}_t$
  \hfill\COMMENT{scalarize timing}
  \STATE Report $o_t$ to TPE
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} top-$K$ configurations by predicted PPA
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:setup}

\textbf{Benchmarks.} Experiments use
ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} circuits with the Nangate45 library.
Ten circuits serve as training data (bp\_fe, bp\_be12, isa\_npu,
bp\_multi, or1200, swerv\_wrapper43, vga\_lcd, ethernet, dft68,
mor1kx); five held-out circuits are used for end-to-end evaluation
(swerv\_wrapper, ariane133, black\_parrot, bp\_be, ariane136). The
test set coincides with LaMPlace's~\cite{lamplace2025} ChiPBench
evaluation circuits, enabling ratio-level comparison using their
published results under the same Hier-RTLMP normalization.
All placements are evaluated through the full OpenROAD flow to
post-route, yielding WNS, TNS, power, and area.

\textbf{Training data.} Per training circuit, $M = 500$ placements are
generated by varying DREAMPlace mixed-size hyperparameters (target
density, density weight, learning rate, random seed) and evaluated
through the OpenROAD flow to post-GRT, producing ${\sim}$5{,}000
training pairs. Configurations that fail DREAMPlace convergence are
discarded and resampled until $M$ successful placements per circuit are
collected. A separate set of RTLMP weight configurations is
evaluated to post-CTS, post-placement STA, and post-DRT for the label
fidelity experiments (Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}).

\textbf{Baselines.}
The upper section of Table~\ref{tab:main} compares against prior
placement methods:
\emph{Hier-RTLMP}, OpenROAD's hierarchical macro placer and the
ChiPBench baseline (normalized to 1.00);
\emph{DREAMPlace}~\cite{lin2019dreamplace}, analytical placement with
default configuration; and
\emph{LaMPlace}~\cite{lamplace2025}, cross-stage prediction with
WireMask-EA evolutionary search (published ChiPBench results from
Appendix~B.1, marked $^\dagger$).
The lower section evaluates PPASurrogate within the same DREAMPlace
configuration space:
\emph{Random Search} samples 50 configurations uniformly and selects the
best by post-route PPA (an oracle upper bound on uninformed search);
\emph{Proxy-BO} runs TPE over 50 trials using AutoDMP's post-placement
proxies (HPWL $+$ cell density $+$ RUDY congestion) as the BO
objective---this isolates the effect of proxy choice from search strategy;
\emph{PPASurrogate-select} picks the best from $K{=}500$ pre-generated
candidates via the surrogate (Eq.~\eqref{eq:select}); and
\emph{PPASurrogate-BO} runs surrogate-guided BO over 50 trials
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:bo}).

\textbf{Configuration space.} Five DREAMPlace parameters: target
density, density weight (log-scale), learning rate (log-scale),
stopping overflow threshold, and wirelength model. The remaining
parameters are fixed at defaults.

\textbf{Metrics.} Post-route WNS (ps), TNS (ns), power (mW), and area
($\mu$m$^2$), normalized by Hier-RTLMP (lower is better).

\textbf{Training.} Adam optimizer (lr $= 5 \times 10^{-4}$, weight
decay $10^{-5}$), 200 epochs, batch size 32, ranking loss weight
$\lambda_r = 0.1$. Batches are grouped by circuit for ranking pairs.
Results: mean $\pm$ std over 3 seeds.

\textbf{Hardware and offline cost.} All training and experiments are
conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU (48\,GB) with an Intel Xeon
Gold 5218R CPU and 64\,GB RAM. DREAMPlace placement runs on GPU
(${\sim}24$\,s per configuration); post-GRT evaluation runs on CPU with
16 concurrent OpenROAD processes (${\sim}3$--$18$\,min per sample
depending on circuit size). The 5{,}000 training labels are generated
in ${\sim}48$\,h wall-clock (placement and GRT evaluation pipelined).
Model training takes ${\sim}15$\,min on the same GPU. The offline cost
is amortized: once generated, the same dataset and trained model serve
all downstream BO runs and new circuits without additional labeling.

\textbf{Prior methods (upper section).} DREAMPlace with default
configuration degrades timing relative to Hier-RTLMP on every circuit
(average WNS $1.99\times$), consistent with the HPWL-to-PPA disconnect
reported in ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025}.
Figure~\ref{fig:placement} visualizes this on swerv\_wrapper: the
default DREAMPlace achieves the lowest HPWL yet produces the worst
post-route timing, while PPASurrogate-BO finds a configuration with
substantially better timing despite higher wirelength.
LaMPlace reduces this gap
(average WNS $1.32\times$) through cross-stage prediction but remains
above the baseline on all circuits.

\textbf{Configuration optimization (lower section).} Random search over
50 configurations already improves over the default ($1.25\times$ vs
$1.99\times$), showing that the default is far from optimal. However,
random search remains above the Hier-RTLMP baseline because without a
quality-aware objective, it cannot consistently find good configurations.

Proxy-BO, which replaces the random objective with AutoDMP's
post-placement proxies (HPWL $+$ density $+$ RUDY), reduces average WNS
from $1.25\times$ to $1.10\times$---the proxies carry useful signal and
BO exploits it. Yet Proxy-BO still fails to beat Hier-RTLMP on three of
five circuits because pre-route proxies cannot capture the timing effects
introduced by clock tree synthesis and routing. The gap is most
pronounced on ariane133 ($1.48\times$ WNS), where the complex macro
arrangement makes post-route behavior particularly hard to predict from
placement-level metrics alone.

PPASurrogate-BO, using the learned post-route surrogate as objective, achieves average
WNS of $0.88 \pm 0.02$ and TNS of $0.79 \pm 0.03$ using the same 50
trials, corresponding to 12\% and 21\% improvements over Hier-RTLMP.
The gain is circuit-dependent: 5\% WNS on ariane133 (132 macros, where
hierarchical placement is already strong) versus 19\% on bp\_be (10
macros, where configuration tuning has more impact). PPASurrogate-select
achieves comparable timing ($0.91$ WNS, $0.84$ TNS) but requires
$10\times$ more candidate evaluations. Power remains within 2\% of
the baseline across all methods and circuits, as expected: timing
optimization primarily redistributes cells to reduce critical-path
delay, which does not significantly alter total switching activity.

\textbf{Note on comparisons.} LaMPlace results ($^\dagger$) are from
their published ChiPBench evaluation~\cite{lamplace2025}, normalized by
the same Hier-RTLMP baseline (verified within 2\% on two circuits).
LaMPlace optimizes macro positions via evolutionary search, whereas
PPASurrogate optimizes DREAMPlace configuration---complementary
approaches on different search spaces.
AutoDMP~\cite{autodmp2023} uses different benchmarks (TILOS) and a
commercial tool (Cadence Innovus), so direct PPA comparison is
infeasible. The Proxy-BO baseline reproduces AutoDMP's core
idea---BO with HPWL, density, and RUDY proxies---under our setup; the
gap ($1.10\times$ vs $0.88\times$ average WNS) quantifies the value of
post-route surrogate guidance over post-placement proxies.

top-5.

\textbf{Hyperparameter importance.}
Functional ANOVA (fANOVA) analysis on the 50 BO trials shows target density accounts for
38\% of objective variance, followed by density weight (28\%) and
learning rate (19\%). This is consistent with the density--wirelength
trade-off in analytical placement~\cite{lu2015eplace}: target density
directly controls how aggressively cells are spread, which determines
congestion and timing.

\textbf{Seed robustness.}
The best BO configuration for each test circuit is re-run with 5
placement seeds. Normalized post-route WNS (mean $\pm$ std): bp\_be
$0.82 \pm 0.03$, black\_parrot $0.85 \pm 0.03$, swerv\_wrapper
$0.89 \pm 0.02$, ariane136 $0.92 \pm 0.03$, ariane133
$0.96 \pm 0.02$. The low variance across seeds confirms that PPA
quality is intrinsic to the configuration rather than seed-specific.

slight fidelity gap.

$0.74$ for TNS, with Kendall $\tau = 0.58$ and $0.55$ respectively.
Top-5 accuracy, the fraction of the predictor's top-5 picks that
appear in the ground-truth top-5, averages 68\% compared to 2\%
expected by chance from a pool of 500.

\textbf{Cross-circuit transfer.}
Leave-one-circuit-out (LOCO) cross-validation trains on 9 circuits and
evaluates the held-out circuit (Figure~\ref{fig:generalization}(b)). $\tau$
ranges from $0.07 \pm 0.03$ (isa\_npu) to $0.28 \pm 0.02$ (mor1kx).
Circuits with related designs in the training set (e.g., BlackParrot
variants: bp\_fe $\tau = 0.26$) transfer more readily than
architecturally distinct ones. These moderate $\tau$ values nonetheless
translate to practical utility: across all 10 folds, the LOCO predictor
selects a top-10\% placement 64\% of the time (vs 10\% by chance) and a
top-25\% placement 82\% of the time. For BO, the surrogate only needs
to distinguish good regions of the configuration space from bad ones,
not rank individual configurations perfectly, and the convergence
results in Figure~\ref{fig:generalization}(a) confirm that surrogate-guided
BO consistently outperforms random search even under this setting.

\textbf{Cross-placer transfer.}
Because the predictor takes a finished placement as input rather than
any placer-specific intermediate state, the same trained model can
score placements from a different algorithm. To test this, we apply the
DREAMPlace-trained predictor to 20 RTLMP configurations per circuit
from the fidelity study in Section~\ref{sec:label_fidelity}.
Table~\ref{tab:cross_placer} reports the results. Cross-placer $\rho$
averages 0.63 for WNS and 0.58 for TNS, lower than the within-placer
accuracy (0.89 / 0.87) but well above zero and statistically
significant on all circuits ($p < 0.01$). The gap is expected:
DREAMPlace and RTLMP produce structurally different placements
(analytical gradient descent vs hierarchical partitioning), and the
predictor was trained exclusively on the former. Nevertheless, the
spatial and connectivity patterns that distinguish good placements from
bad ones transfer across paradigms because they reflect physical design
principles common to both approaches.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented PPASurrogate, a framework that enhances chip placement
through surrogate-guided configuration optimization. Two findings
underpin the approach: post-global-routing labels consistently preserve
placement rankings ($\rho \geq 0.78$ across 10 circuits), while the
pre-route and HPWL metrics used by prior work do not; and a mixed-size
representation capturing both netlist connectivity and spatial density
substantially outperforms the macro-only encoding used in existing
predictors.

The resulting surrogate supports both candidate selection and Bayesian
optimization over a placer's configuration space. On five circuits
unseen during training, PPASurrogate-BO achieves 12\% WNS and 21\% TNS
improvement over the Hier-RTLMP baseline in 50 trials, while all
prior AI placement methods remain above the baseline on the same
benchmark. Because the predictor evaluates a finished placement rather
than placer-specific state, it transfers across placement paradigms:
trained on DREAMPlace data alone, the same model ranks RTLMP placements
with $\rho = 0.63$ without retraining.
The main limitation is the one-time cost of generating post-GRT training
labels (${\sim}48$\,h per technology node); validating on advanced nodes
and closing the cross-placer accuracy gap via few-shot fine-tuning are
promising directions for future work.
