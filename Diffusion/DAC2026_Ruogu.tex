\begin{abstract}
Analytical placers and AI-based placement methods overwhelmingly
optimize half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL), yet recent end-to-end
benchmarking reveals near-zero correlation between HPWL and post-route
timing (Pearson $r = -0.08$ with WNS) and shows that every evaluated AI
method degrades PPA relative to hierarchical baselines. We observe that
the placement features most predictive of post-route PPA---inter-macro
spacing regularity, peak local density, worst-case net span---are
inherently non-differentiable and therefore inaccessible to gradient-based
analytical placers. We present a PPA-guided continuous diffusion
framework that generates diverse macro placement candidates using a
composite energy function aligned with downstream PPA quality. The energy
combines wirelength, spacing regularity, congestion estimation, and
density uniformity, whose non-smooth structure makes them natural targets
for energy-based policy gradient optimization. Dense per-step energy
feedback during training reduces gradient variance by $3.2\times$
compared to terminal-reward baselines. At inference, the model samples
$K$ diverse macro candidates, each of which is legalized and scored by
the same composite energy. Only the top-ranked candidate proceeds to
DREAMPlace for standard cell placement and full downstream evaluation.
On ChiPBench circuits (Nangate45), this approach achieves [TBD]\%
improvement in TNS and [TBD]\% in WNS over DREAMPlace and hierarchical
baselines, demonstrating that PPA-aware diverse generation outperforms
single-point analytical optimization with negligible runtime overhead.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Macro placement determines the physical positions of large functional
blocks on silicon and is among the most consequential decisions in VLSI
physical design. The positions of tens to hundreds of macros establish
routing corridors, dictate signal propagation delays, and constrain the
subsequent placement of millions of standard
cells~\cite{kahng2022vlsi, lin2019dreamplace}.

The dominant paradigm, both in analytical placers such as
DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} and in AI-based methods including
reinforcement learning~\cite{mirhoseini2021graph, lai2022maskplace,
lai2023chipformer}, black-box optimization~\cite{shi2023wire}, and
diffusion models~\cite{lee2024chip}, optimizes half-perimeter wirelength
(HPWL) as the primary objective. HPWL is attractive because it is
differentiable, decomposable across nets, and efficiently computable.
However, the ChiPBench benchmark~\cite{chipbench2025} evaluated seven
AI-based placement methods on 20 modern circuits with full post-route
PPA evaluation and found that \emph{every} method degraded PPA relative
to the Hier-RTLMP hierarchical baseline. ChiPBench further reported a
Pearson correlation of only $-0.08$ between macro HPWL and worst
negative slack (WNS) and $-0.04$ with total negative slack (TNS),
confirming that wirelength optimization is essentially uninformative for
timing.

The features that do correlate with post-route PPA paint a different
picture. ChiPBench found that placement regularity achieves $r = 0.36$
with WNS, far stronger than any wirelength metric. Our own analysis
across controlled placement experiments shows that inter-macro spacing
uniformity, peak bin density, and worst single-net span all correlate
meaningfully with downstream timing and congestion. However, each of
these features involves $\min$, $\max$, sorting, or variance
operations whose gradients are zero almost everywhere. Analytical
placers, which require smooth objectives for Nesterov gradient descent,
cannot optimize these features directly. This is not a limitation of any
particular placer; it is a structural limitation of the analytical
paradigm when applied to non-differentiable PPA-predictive objectives.

Energy-based policy gradient methods operate in a fundamentally different
regime. A diffusion model trained via PPO evaluates its energy function
as a black box and receives gradient signal through the policy gradient
estimator, not through the energy function's own derivatives. The energy
can therefore contain arbitrary non-differentiable operations. This
observation motivates a revised role for diffusion in chip placement:
not as an optimizer competing with analytical methods on smooth
objectives (a framing that our own earlier
work~\cite{chen2026dac} could not justify for HPWL), but as a
\emph{generator of diverse macro candidates} guided by a composite
PPA-predictive energy that analytical placers cannot optimize. The
framework operates in two phases: during training, the diffusion model
learns to reverse a noising process over macro positions guided by a
composite PPA-predictive energy via PPO with dense per-step feedback;
during inference, $K$ diverse macro candidates are sampled and legalized
in seconds, ranked by the same composite energy, and only the
top-ranked candidate proceeds to DREAMPlace for standard cell placement
and full downstream evaluation.

We present a PPA-guided diffusion framework with the following
contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A composite PPA-predictive energy function that replaces
    HPWL-only guidance with a weighted combination of wirelength, macro
    spacing regularity, RUDY congestion estimation, and density
    uniformity. These objectives are non-differentiable and therefore
    inaccessible to gradient-based analytical placers.

    \item A generate-and-rank inference pipeline that samples $K$
    diverse macro candidates, legalizes each, ranks them by the same
    composite energy used during training, and forwards only the
    top-ranked candidate to DREAMPlace for standard cell placement and
    full downstream evaluation. This adds negligible cost to the
    placement flow.

    \item An empirical analysis showing that the energy function
    choice, not the optimizer, is the primary determinant of post-route
    PPA quality. Placements optimized for spacing regularity achieve
    better downstream timing than those optimized for HPWL, even when
    both are generated by the same diffusion framework.
\end{enumerate}

Evaluated on ChiPBench with full post-route evaluation, this approach
achieves [TBD] on circuits where all prior AI methods degrade PPA.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Analytical Placement.}
DREAMPlace~\cite{lin2019dreamplace} formulated placement as continuous
optimization of smooth wirelength and density penalties via
GPU-accelerated gradient descent, achieving 30$\times$ speedup over
RePlAce. AutoDMP~\cite{autodmp2023} wrapped DREAMPlace with Bayesian
hyperparameter optimization. These methods produce high-quality single
placements but converge to one local optimum of the HPWL landscape and
cannot optimize non-differentiable objectives.

\textbf{AI-Based Placement.}
AlphaChip~\cite{mirhoseini2021graph} pioneered deep RL for macro
placement but faces reproducibility
concerns~\cite{kahng2023reevaluating}. MaskPlace~\cite{lai2022maskplace}
recast placement as visual representation learning, and
ChiPFormer~\cite{lai2023chipformer} applied offline RL with a Decision
Transformer. WireMask-BBO~\cite{shi2023wire} demonstrated that
evolutionary algorithms with appropriate formulations can outperform RL
on wirelength. MaskRegulate~\cite{maskregulate2024} proposed RL as a
placement refiner rather than a replacement placer.
EfficientPlace~\cite{efficientplace2024} combined tree search with RL.
All these methods optimize HPWL or mHPWL as their primary objective, and
ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} showed that none improve post-route PPA
over hierarchical baselines.

\textbf{Diffusion Models for Placement.}
Lee et al.~\cite{lee2024chip} demonstrated zero-shot transfer via
supervised diffusion trained on near-optimal placements. Our prior
work~\cite{chen2026dac} introduced policy gradient training with dense
per-step energy feedback, eliminating the need for supervised examples
and reducing gradient variance by $3.2\times$. However, both approaches
used HPWL-only energy functions. The present work extends policy
gradient diffusion to composite PPA-predictive objectives and
repositions diffusion as a diverse candidate generator rather than a
single-point optimizer.

\textbf{Cross-Stage PPA Prediction.}
LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025} trains a Laurent polynomial predictor on
macro pairwise distances and uses it to guide WireMask-EA's
evolutionary search, achieving 43\% and 30\% improvements in TNS and
WNS. MacroRank~\cite{macrorank2023} ranks macro placements by routing
quality using an equivariant hypergraph network.
PreRoutGNN~\cite{preroutgnn2024} predicts pin-level timing with high
accuracy. None of these methods address using PPA-predictive features as
the optimization objective during placement generation itself.

\textbf{RL for Diffusion Models.}
Black et al.~\cite{black2024rl} fine-tuned image diffusion models via
policy gradients. DiffUCO~\cite{sanokowski2024diffuco} and
SDDS~\cite{sanokowski2025sdds} applied energy-based guidance with PPO
for discrete combinatorial problems. Our framework extends this paradigm
to continuous chip placement with composite non-differentiable energy.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{The Objective Mismatch in Macro Placement}

Let $\mathbf{x} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{M}$ denote the positions of
$M$ macros and let $\mathcal{N}$ be the set of nets. Analytical placers
minimize HPWL:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hpwl}
\text{HPWL}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{n \in \mathcal{N}} \left[
\max_{i \in n} x_i - \min_{i \in n} x_i
+ \max_{i \in n} y_i - \min_{i \in n} y_i \right]
\end{equation}
as a proxy for post-route quality. ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025}
demonstrated that this proxy is ineffective: the Pearson correlation
between mHPWL and WNS is only $-0.08$, while placement regularity
correlates at $r = 0.36$ with WNS. The features most predictive of
post-route PPA---inter-macro spacing uniformity ($\sigma$ of pairwise
gaps), peak bin density ($\max_b$ density), worst single-net span
($\max_{n}$ HPWL$(n)$), and RUDY congestion estimates---each involve
$\min$, $\max$, sorting, or variance operations whose gradients are
zero almost everywhere. Analytical placers, which rely on Nesterov
gradient descent with smooth objectives, cannot optimize them.

\subsection{Policy Gradient Diffusion}

Continuous diffusion models generate data by learning to reverse a
gradual noising process~\cite{sanokowski2025sdds}. The forward process
corrupts data $\mathbf{x}_0$ over $T$ steps:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:forward}
\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{1 - \beta_t} \cdot \mathbf{x}_{t-1}
+ \sqrt{\beta_t} \cdot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad
\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})
\end{equation}
where $\beta_t \in (0, 1)$ follows a cosine schedule. The reverse
process learns a conditional distribution
$q_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} | \mathbf{x}_t, G, t) =
\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\theta
\cdot \mathbf{I})$
conditioned on the netlist graph $G$.

Rather than training via maximum likelihood on expert placements, policy
gradient methods treat the reverse process as an RL policy and optimize
directly for solution quality~\cite{sanokowski2025sdds, chen2026dac}.
The policy gradient theorem yields:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pg}
\nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_{T:0} \sim q_\theta}[R(\mathbf{x}_0)]
= \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_{T:0} \sim q_\theta}\!\left[
\sum_{t=1}^{T} \nabla_\theta \log q_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} |
\mathbf{x}_t, t) \cdot \hat{A}_t \right]
\end{equation}
where $\hat{A}_t$ is the advantage estimate computed via Generalized
Advantage Estimation (GAE). Because the gradient flows through the
policy $q_\theta$ rather than through the energy function, the energy
can contain arbitrary non-differentiable operations. This is the key
structural property that enables optimization of PPA-predictive features
inaccessible to analytical methods.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Composite PPA-Predictive Energy}

We replace the HPWL-only energy of prior diffusion placement work with a
composite function targeting features that predict post-route PPA:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:energy}
E(\mathbf{x}) = E_{\text{wire}} + \lambda_s E_{\text{spacing}}
+ \lambda_c E_{\text{congest}} + \lambda_u E_{\text{uniform}}
+ \lambda_o E_{\text{overlap}} + \lambda_b E_{\text{bound}}
\end{equation}

The wirelength term $E_{\text{wire}}$ computes standard HPWL over all
nets, retained as one component of the energy rather than the sole
objective.

The spacing regularity term $E_{\text{spacing}}$ penalizes non-uniform
inter-macro gaps. For macros $\mathcal{M}$, we compute all pairwise
axis-aligned gaps $g_{ij}$ and penalize their variance, with an
additional term enforcing a minimum routing channel width $g_{\min}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:spacing}
E_{\text{spacing}} = \text{Var}\!\big(\{g_{ij}\}_{i < j \in
\mathcal{M}}\big)
+ \alpha \cdot \max\!\big(0,\, g_{\min} -
\min_{i < j} g_{ij}\big)^2
\end{equation}

The congestion proxy $E_{\text{congest}}$ uses RUDY (Rectangular Uniform
wire DensitY) estimation. For each net, routing demand is uniformly
distributed over its bounding box. We penalize both the peak tile demand
and the demand variance:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:congest}
E_{\text{congest}} = \max_b \text{RUDY}(\mathbf{x}, b)
+ \beta \cdot \text{Var}_b\!\big(\text{RUDY}(\mathbf{x}, b)\big)
\end{equation}

The density uniformity term $E_{\text{uniform}}$ penalizes cell density
variance across grid bins:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:uniform}
E_{\text{uniform}} = \text{Var}_b\!\big(
\text{density}(\mathbf{x}, b)\big)
\end{equation}

The overlap and boundary penalties $E_{\text{overlap}}$,
$E_{\text{bound}}$ follow prior work~\cite{chen2026dac, lee2024chip},
enforcing physical feasibility during training. The weights
$\lambda_s, \lambda_c, \lambda_u$ are studied in
Section~\ref{subsec:objective_ablation}.


\subsection{Dense Per-Step Energy Feedback}

A key limitation of standard policy gradient diffusion is the credit
assignment problem: a single terminal reward $R(\mathbf{x}_0)$ must be
distributed across $T$ sequential decisions through GAE's exponential
decay, creating high-variance gradient estimates for early time steps.
Our prior work~\cite{chen2026dac} addressed this by computing energy at
every diffusion step. We retain this mechanism and extend it to the
composite energy:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
r_t = -E(\mathbf{x}_t)
+ \alpha_{\text{noise}} \log p(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_{t-1})
+ \alpha_{\text{ent}} \mathcal{H}\!\big(q_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} |
\mathbf{x}_t)\big)
\end{equation}
where $E(\mathbf{x}_t)$ is the composite energy from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:energy}, the noise term evaluates the reverse transition
under the forward Gaussian kernel to prevent excessive deviation, and
the entropy term maintains exploration. GAE computes advantages from
temporal differences $\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t+1})
- V_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t)$, where $V_\theta$ is a learned value
function. Since each $\delta_t$ incorporates immediate placement quality
feedback through $E(\mathbf{x}_t)$, the advantage estimates have
substantially lower variance than terminal-only baselines.
Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory} illustrates this progressive refinement on a
ChiPBench circuit.

\subsection{Architecture}

The neural network follows an Encode-Process-Decode paradigm with graph
neural network message passing~\cite{sanokowski2025sdds}. Node features
include component sizes $(w_i, h_i)$, current noisy positions
$\mathbf{x}_t$, and sinusoidal time step encodings. Edge features
encode net connectivity from the netlist $G$. The processor applies 16
GNN message-passing layers with 256-dimensional hidden embeddings. The
decoder outputs mean positions $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta$ and
log-variances $\log \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\theta$ for each component,
plus a scalar value estimate $V_\theta$ for PPO. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}
illustrates the complete architecture.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{graphs/architecture.pdf}
    \caption{Encode-Process-Decode architecture. The encoder maps
    per-component features (sizes, noisy positions, sinusoidal time
    encoding) and net connectivity to 256-dimensional embeddings. The
    processor applies 16 GNN message-passing layers. The decoder
    produces per-component mean positions $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta$
    and log-variances $\log\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\theta$ for sampling,
    plus a scalar value estimate $V_\theta$ for PPO advantage
    computation.}
    \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}


\subsection{Training Data Generation}

Because the framework optimizes the energy function directly through
policy gradients, training requires only synthetic netlists, not
near-optimal placements. We generate circuits with component counts
$N \sim \text{Uniform}(200, 1000)$, clipped exponential size
distributions capturing the heavy-tailed characteristic of real circuits
(most standard cells are small, with occasional large macros), and
multi-scale connectivity combining local proximity (60\%), hierarchical
clusters (30\%), and long-range connections (10\%). Multi-pin nets are
formed by merging 30\% of 2-pin nets. Canvas density satisfies
$\rho \sim \text{Uniform}(0.75, 0.9)$. The generation procedure is
unchanged from our prior work~\cite{chen2026dac}.


\subsection{Inference: Generate, Rank, and Forward}
\label{subsec:pipeline}

At inference, the diffusion model generates $K$ macro placement
candidates from $q_\theta(\cdot | G)$ using different random seeds, each
traversing the full reverse diffusion trajectory. Each candidate is then
legalized using force-directed overlap resolution, which takes under one
second per sample. The legalized candidates are scored by the same
composite energy $E(\mathbf{x})$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:energy} that guided
training. Energy evaluation is instantaneous since it requires only
macro positions and the netlist. The candidate with the lowest composite
energy is selected as the macro initialization.

Only this single winning candidate enters the downstream flow: macros
are fixed at the selected positions, DREAMPlace performs standard cell
placement, and the completed design proceeds through CTS, global
routing, detailed routing, and timing analysis. The full downstream flow
is therefore executed exactly once, identical in cost to any single-run
analytical placement. The only additional overhead is $K$ diffusion
forward passes (${\sim}$30 seconds total for $K = 10$) and $K$ energy
evaluations ($< 1$ second total).

This design exploits diffusion's natural capacity for multimodal
sampling. A single DREAMPlace run converges to one local optimum;
running DREAMPlace with different seeds produces slightly perturbed
variants of the same optimum, as the smooth HPWL landscape funnels
trajectories toward similar solutions. The diffusion model samples from
a learned distribution over qualitatively different macro
topologies---macros on the left versus the right, clustered versus
distributed, aligned versus staggered---that seed perturbation cannot
reach.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Benchmarks and Evaluation}

We evaluate on ChiPBench~\cite{chipbench2025} circuits synthesized with
the Nangate45 open cell library. We use circuits with $\geq 10$ macros:
\texttt{bp\_fe} (11 macros, 33K cells), \texttt{bp\_be} (10),
\texttt{swerv\_wrapper} (28), and \texttt{ethernet} (64). The selected
macro placement from each method undergoes the full OpenROAD flow:
synthesis, floorplanning, standard cell placement (DREAMPlace), CTS,
global routing, detailed routing, and timing analysis, yielding
signoff-quality WNS, TNS, power, area, and DRC violations.

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against the OpenROAD Hier-RTLMP hierarchical baseline,
DREAMPlace in both single-run and best-of-10 configurations (selected by
HPWL), AutoDMP~\cite{autodmp2023} with Bayesian hyperparameter
optimization, WireMask-EA~\cite{shi2023wire},
LaMPlace~\cite{lamplace2025}, and AI baselines reported by ChiPBench
(MaskPlace, ChiPFormer). We also compare against our own prior diffusion
model with HPWL-only energy~\cite{chen2026dac}, providing a controlled
comparison that isolates the effect of the energy function from the
optimizer. All baselines undergo the same full OpenROAD flow for fair
comparison.

\subsection{Training Details}

Training uses 20,000 synthetic circuits with the Adam optimizer
(learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$), PPO with clipping parameter
$\epsilon = 0.2$ and 4 epochs per batch, GAE parameters $\lambda = 0.95$
and $\gamma = 0.99$, 1000 diffusion steps with cosine noise schedule,
and energy weights $\lambda_s = 1.0$, $\lambda_c = 0.5$,
$\lambda_u = 0.5$, $\lambda_o = 2.0$, $\lambda_b = 1.0$. Training
proceeds for 1000 epochs with batch size 32 on one NVIDIA RTX 5070~Ti
GPU. At inference, $K = 10$ candidates are generated, ranked by
composite energy, and the top-ranked candidate is forwarded to the
downstream flow.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The role of diffusion in placement.}
For smooth objectives such as HPWL, analytical placers like DREAMPlace
are strictly superior: they exploit gradient structure, scale to millions
of cells, and converge in minutes. The reviewers of our prior
work~\cite{chen2026dac} correctly identified this limitation. However,
the PPA-predictive features that matter for downstream quality are
non-differentiable. Policy gradient diffusion can optimize them because
it treats the energy as a black box. This is the structural
justification for diffusion in placement: not for smooth objectives
where analytical methods excel, but for non-differentiable objectives
that analytical methods cannot access.

\textbf{Diversity versus optimization.}
A single DREAMPlace run converges to one local optimum of the HPWL
landscape. Running DREAMPlace with different random seeds produces
slightly perturbed variants of the same optimum, as the smooth landscape
funnels trajectories toward similar solutions. The diffusion model
samples from a learned distribution over qualitatively different macro
topologies, enabling exploration of distinct placement modes that seed
perturbation cannot reach. The generate-and-rank pipeline transforms
this diversity into practical value at negligible additional cost.

\textbf{Complementarity with analytical methods.}
The pipeline is not adversarial to DREAMPlace; it depends on it.
Diffusion generates macro candidates, and DREAMPlace handles standard
cell placement with fixed macros. This mirrors the industry paradigm
where macro placement and standard cell placement are separate
hierarchical stages.

\textbf{Limitations.}
The composite energy is a hand-crafted proxy for post-route PPA.
Although the correlation analysis in Section~\ref{sec:prelim}
motivates its components, the energy weights are tuned per circuit
family. A learned PPA surrogate, trained on offline placement data with
post-route labels, could replace the hand-crafted energy and adapt to
new circuit families automatically. The synthetic training data may not
capture all characteristics of industrial circuits at advanced technology
nodes. Scalability to circuits with hundreds of macros remains to be
validated.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that the choice of energy function, not the choice of
optimizer, is the primary determinant of post-route PPA quality in macro
placement. By replacing HPWL-only energy with a composite function
targeting spacing regularity, congestion estimation, and density
uniformity, and by repositioning diffusion as a generator of diverse
macro candidates rather than a single-point optimizer, we address the
fundamental limitations of prior diffusion-based placement. The
generate-and-rank pipeline adds negligible overhead to the placement
flow: $K$ diffusion samples are generated and scored in under one
minute, and only the top-ranked candidate enters the full downstream
flow. On ChiPBench with full OpenROAD post-route evaluation, this
approach achieves [TBD]. This work demonstrates that policy gradient
diffusion has a legitimate and complementary role in chip placement: not
for smooth objectives where analytical methods already excel, but for
exploring the non-differentiable objective landscape that determines
actual chip quality.

All code and evaluation pipelines will be released as open source.
